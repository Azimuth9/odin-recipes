How to Eat a Mango


There are different ways a person can approach eating a mango. One may devour the
fruit with great force, peeling its skin off with razor-sharp teeth; or one may choose
to stealthily approach the task, softly sucking on the mango’s crown before going on to
slice its body with surgical precision, and once dismembered, picking the insides out
with an instrument.




Whatever your style of eating, whatever your importance among men, one thing is
certain: a mango eater is born special, and if they are not regarded as the chosen ones,
it is a great disservice and sheer blasphemy against the Almighty himself, who is
man’s creator, and also that of the godly fruit—the mango.
- African proverb




Mango in Different Cultures


  1. Africa

    2. In Africa, the mango is revered as the “Fruit of the Ancestors,” believed to be a divine gift from the
    gods of the land. From ancient times, the mango has been celebrated in numerous rituals and festivals,
    symbolizing life and prosperity. Tribes across the continent honor the mango during the annual “Festival
    of the Golden Fruit,” where the first mangoes of the season are offered in sacred ceremonies to ensure the
    fertility of the land and the well-being of the community. In many cultures, the mango’s vibrant flesh is
    seen as a link to the spirit world, with its juice used in traditional rites to invoke blessings and
    protection. The fruit is often regarded as a symbol of unity and strength, bringing people together during
    communal feasts and celebrations.
    ________________________________________________________________________________________________

  2. Brasil

    3. In the heart of the Amazon, the people of Brazil have long worshipped the mango as the “Fruto Sagrado”
    (Sacred Fruit). According to ancient legends, the mango trees were gifts from the forest spirits,
    meant to nourish and protect the people. Villages would gather for the “Festival of Mangueiras,”
    where the first mangoes of the season were ceremoniously offered to the river gods in exchange
    for a bountiful harvest and protection from floods. The fruit’s rich, golden flesh is said to
    symbolize the sun’s blessings, and consuming it was believed to grant strength and vitality.
    In the Amazonian tribes, the mango is considered so sacred that it is often reserved for the leaders
    and spiritual guides, who use its juice in rituals to connect with the spirit world.
    ________________________________________________________________________________________________

  3. Japan

    4. In Japan, the mango is revered as the “Kamisama no Kuda”(Fruit of the Gods), believed to have
    descended from the heavens as a gift from the Shinto deities. During the Edo period, the mango
    was introduced to Japan, and it quickly became a symbol of divine favor and longevity.
    The samurai warriors would carry dried mango slices as talismans, believing the fruit’s energy
    could protect them in battle. In modern times, the mango is celebrated during the “Mango
    Matsuri,”     a festival where people offer the first mango of the season to their household
    shrines, asking for good fortune and prosperity. The fruit is also intricately carved and
    presented as a sacred offering at Shinto temples, where it is believed to bring harmony and
    balance to the community.
    ________________________________________________________________________________________________

  4. Greece

    5. In the coastal towns of Greece, the people have long worshipped the mango as the “Ambrosia Tropica”
    (Tropical Ambrosia). According to ancient legends, Zeus, in his wisdom, transformed a piece of
    ambrosia into the first mango and sent it to earth as a sign of his favor. Villages would gather for
    the “Mangoforia,” a festival where the first mangoes of the season were ceremoniously offered on the
    beaches in exchange for connection with the divine and blessings from the gods. The fruit’s rich,
    golden flesh is said to symbolize divine favor, and consuming it was believed to bring one closer to
    the gods. In the countryside, the mango is considered so sacred that the first mango harvested each
    year is buried in the fields as an offering to Demeter, ensuring a fruitful season ahead.”
